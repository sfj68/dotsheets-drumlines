Characters
Primary Stats

Each player character has three primary stats: Brawn, Brain, and Brisk.

Brawn

Brawn is connected to the strength and endurance of a character and is typically associated with martial-based classes, who use their strength and weaponry to turn the tide of battle. Brawn is associated with how many Hit Points (HP) you receive.

Brain

Brain is connected to the general intelligence & wisdom of a character and is typically associated with magic-based classes, who harness their minds to control the world around them. The Brain stat is associated with how many Action Points you receive.

Brisk

Brisk is connected to the finesse and dexterity of a character and is typically associated with thieves, vagabonds, and travellers who spend their lives sneaking around or using their personality to navigate through the world. The Brisk stat is associated with your character's Dodge.

Secondary Stats

In addition, each character will need to roll for their secondary stats. Your Health, Action Points, and Speed are determined by the following formulas:

Health
HP = 20 + (Brawn × 5)

Hit Points (HP) track how much damage you have taken during combat. The impact to your hit points is based on damage.

Action Points
AP = 1 + Brain

Action Points (AP) track how many times you are able to use your character's special ability. Action Points reset at the beginning of an encounter.

Speed
Speed = 6

Each character starts with a value of 6 Speed. When in combat, your speed tells you how many spaces you can move.

Action Points

Your character can use action points to help change the course of events throughout the game. You can ask the game master at any time to spend an action point in order to do some sort of maneuver that makes things a little more interesting.

For example, if you notice that a series of three enemies are in a straight line, you could ask the game master to use an action point to have your attack hit all of the enemies in that line. Your environment also may require you to use action points to help you succeed on certain tasks — for instance, you could spend an action point to imbue your weapon with holy light to defeat the demon lord.

The game master will need to approve any improvised use of action points.
AP Actions (1 AP each)
Radial Attack
Hit all enemies within 1 unit of your attack target.
Piercing Attack
Hit all enemies in a straight line from your target, up to 3 units away.
Helping Hand
Assist an ally's next roll — they add +1d6 and take the better result.
Scan
Ask the GM one honest question about an enemy or the environment (weaknesses, traps, hidden exits, etc.).
Brace
Before an incoming attack, treat the damage as half (rounds up) regardless of your dodge roll.
Taunt
Force a nearby enemy to target you on their next turn instead of another player.

Special Abilities

In addition to the standard AP Actions above, each character gets a Special Ability. This will be determined at the time of character creation and doesn't change in the course of an adventure.

This ability is specific to your character and should emphasize something unique about them. Using your special ability will typically use more action points than a standard action, usually at least 2 AP.

Example Special Abilities
Do-Over (2 AP)
Reroll any check or attack roll.
50/50 (2 AP)
Instead of normal dice rules: 1, 2, 3 = Failure; 4, 5, 6 = Success.
Defend (2 AP)
Take the damage from an attack meant for another player.

Combat Stat

Once you have filled out your character's stats, you will need to choose one of your primary stats to be your character's combat stat. You will use either your Brawn, Brisk, or Brain to roll for attacks during combat.

Connections

Last but not least, each player character should create a connection with another player character at the table.

These connections can range from the simplest, "we met once at a tavern" to a more complex "we have been sworn bloodbrothers since our entire village was slaughtered by a band of sentient frog people when we were children."

This is where things can get interesting and the world starts to shape itself. Have fun!

Rolling Dice

This game uses a d6-based dicepool system. You will roll a number of d6 dice equal to one of your stats in order to make a check, which will succeed or fail based on how you roll the dice.

You will only use the highest rolled die in your dice pool, so rolling more dice means you are more likely to succeed. Trust me, Cody made a spreadsheet about it.

Checks

Checks occur regularly throughout the game and are how you interact with the world, beyond the scope of the ordinary. It doesn't take a check to talk to a shopkeeper, but if you want to haggle a price down, you will need to earn it.

Check Results
1, 2, 3
Failure — You do not succeed, and there will likely be consequences.
4, 5
Mixed Success — You succeeded, but there will be some consequences as well.
6
Success — You have completed the task you were trying to achieve.

Checks are based on your 3 primary stats: for example if you are trying to win a drinking contest, you might roll a Brawn check. Trying to pick a lock? It's probably a Brisk check. Maybe you're trying to search the area for clues — that's probably a Brain check. The game master can choose what stat they would like you to roll for any check, as situations can vary wildly in an open-ended game.

If you have "0" in your stat, you will roll two dice and take the lower of the two values. There's still a chance you can succeed, it's just very low. You do NOT roll your cool dice for a 0 Stat roll and cannot achieve a critical roll.

Critical Rolls

Each player should have 2d6 in their dice pool that are visually different from the rest of their dice. These are their "Cool Dice." When rolling a check with two or more dice, players should always use their cool dice.

If during any check or combat roll, a player rolls a 6 on both of their cool dice, they have performed a Critical Success.

Critical Success Effects
  • Critical Hit (attack roll): Instead of rolling standard damage, the player rolls 6 + 1d6 damage.
  • Attack of Opportunity (defense roll): The player deals 1d6 damage to the attacking opponent.
  • Out of combat: Typically provides an additional boon or benefit such as additional knowledge or higher rewards.
Critical Failure

Outside of combat, there is potential to roll a Critical Failure if you roll a value of "1" on both of your cool dice. If you roll a critical failure, it is an automatic failure on the task, even if you have rolled a success on one of your other dice.

The only time a Critical Failure will have an impact during combat is during a Recovery Roll.

Combat

Combat in this game is turn-based. These sets of alternating turns are separated into rounds. In each round, all combatants will take a turn, where they can move and take an action. The results of the action will be based upon the result of your rolls.

Combatants may also choose to move again instead of taking a standard action.
Attack Rolls

While players can choose to do many different actions during their turn, the most common thing during combat will be an attack roll. Standard attacks can only be used on a single target. Your stat for your attack roll will vary based on the equipment you have selected.

1, 2, 3
Failure — Your attack misses and there will be consequences.
4, 5
Mixed Success — Your attack hits but there may be consequences.
6
Success — Your attack hits; you will do full damage.
Damage Rolls

If an attack is successful, you roll a damage roll. A standard damage roll is 1d6. Special Abilities or equipment may impact the value of your damage roll.

If damage would end in a partial number, always round up.
Dodge Rolls

On an enemy combatant's turn, they will roll to attack as well. Enemy attacks are successful on a 4, 5, 6, and fail on 1, 2, 3. If an enemy attack roll succeeds, the player(s) it is targeting will need to make a defense roll. Your defense roll is based on your Brisk stat.

1, 2, 3
Failure — You take the full force of the enemy attack.
4, 5
Mixed Success — You get out of the way but sustain half-damage.
6
Success — You successfully dodge, taking no damage.

Marks

Oh hi Mark. When a player loses all of their HP, they take a Mark. Upon taking a mark two things happen:

  1. Subtract one point from a primary stat of your choice (Brawn, Brain, or Brisk).
  2. Reset your HP to Maximum.
Think of it like this: if somebody chops at your legs and that's the blow that would normally kill you, instead of dying you reset your HP and lose 1 point of Brisk, because now you're dealing with a major leg injury. Your secondary stats remain unchanged when you take a mark.

Enemies

Enemies, like player characters, have special abilities that they can sometimes use throughout the game or in the course of battle. While this is not common on standard enemies, the more powerful the foe, the more likely they are to have some shenanigans up their sleeve.
